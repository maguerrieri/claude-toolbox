# A Structured Brainstorming Loop for a Solo Maker + AI Collaborator

> **Vendored grounding** for the `generate` skill — the research the loop is built
> on. It frames the generation→decision handoff as "promote to an ADR"; the skill
> generalizes that to a pluggable **Promotion adapter** (see [`../SKILL.md`](../SKILL.md)),
> so wherever this doc says "ADR," read *whatever decision process you plug in*.
> Examples lean on game content (factions, events) because that's the domain the
> sources studied — the techniques are domain-general.

## TL;DR
- **Build your generation engine on three named, battle-tested methods:** (1) **Morphological Analysis** (Fritz Zwicky's "Zwicky box") to spread factions/events/buildings across orthogonal axes so output never goes samey; (2) **deferred-judgment, quantity-first capture** (Osborn's four rules + Tynan Sylvester's RimWorld "ideas reservoir") to accumulate a pool without deciding anything; and (3) **affinity diagramming / the KJ method** to cluster the pool into themes and find gaps after the fact. This is a genuinely different loop from your ADR decision process.
- **The AI collaboration risk is real and measured: LLMs "mode-collapse" toward homogeneous ideas.** The best-validated countermeasures are Chain-of-Thought "generate→critique-for-sameness→expand" prompting (which cut idea similarity from 0.377 to 0.255, approaching the human-group baseline of 0.243 in a Wharton study), running several *different* persona/prompt passes and pooling them, and NOT seeding the model with example ideas when diversity is the goal.
- **Use a keep/kill/mutate/merge iteration cycle with SCAMPER as the mutation checklist**, and only "promote" an item out of the pool into your ADR decision process when options start to compete for the same slot (the double-diamond diverge→converge handoff). Generation and decision should stay separate modes — mixing them is "driving with the brakes on," which kills both.

## Key Findings

1. **Divergence must be physically separated from convergence.** Every serious ideation framework — Osborn's brainstorming, the Osborn-Parnes Creative Problem Solving process, de Bono's Six Thinking Hats, and the Design Council's Double Diamond — is built on one principle: generate with judgment switched off, evaluate later, and never do both at once.
2. **Morphological analysis is the single highest-value technique for your use case.** Defining orthogonal axes (ideology × geography × what-they-want × what-they-fear) and generating across the combinations is exactly how to produce many factions that are structurally different rather than reskins of one idea.
3. **TTRPG faction design gives you a lightweight content schema:** factions as stakeholders with an agenda, a tier/power level, relationships, and a "clock" toward their goal (Blades in the Dark; Apocalypse World "Fronts"). This doubles as a morphological template.
4. **Random tables / oracles are ideation scaffolds, not decision tools** — TTRPG designers explicitly treat borrowed tables as "idea prompts," not binding mechanisms. That is precisely the posture you want for an AI generator.
5. **AI homogenization is a documented, named failure mode** ("mode collapse" / "Artificial Hivemind") with concrete, measured mitigations from 2024-2026 HCI and computational-creativity research.
6. **Promotion from pool to decision is itself a moment to recognize, not a step to rush.** The pool feeds candidates; when two pooled options can't coexist, that's the trigger for an ADR.

## Details

### Area 1 — Classic structured divergent-thinking techniques

**Osborn's brainstorming (Alex Osborn, *Applied Imagination*, 1953).** Four rules: defer judgment (no criticism during ideation), go for quantity ("quantity breeds quality"), welcome wild ideas, and combine/build on ideas ("freewheeling is welcomed; combination and improvement are sought"). The principle: a large pool raises the odds a rare gem appears, and a judgment-free space lets wild ideas surface. The most-violated rule in practice is "defer judgment," which is the one that matters most. Note the well-documented research caveat: face-to-face groups often *under*-produce relative to the same number of people working alone ("nominal groups"), due to production blocking and evaluation apprehension — which is exactly why the written/asynchronous variants below exist, and why a solo dev + AI sidesteps the classic group losses entirely.

**Brainwriting / Method 6-3-5 (Bernd Rohrbach, 1968).** Six participants each write 3 ideas in 5 minutes on a sheet, then pass the sheet on; the next person *builds on the existing ideas*; six rounds yields 108 ideas in 30 minutes. The build-on-prior mechanic is the key structural feature for iteration: each pass is seeded by what came before. The solo-AI analogue is obvious — the AI is your "next seat," handed the sheet and told to build, not repeat. Related variants: the gallery method, brainwriting pool, and the "pass the idea" mechanics.

**Nominal Group Technique (Delbecq & Van de Ven, 1975).** Five steps: silent independent idea generation → round-robin sharing (recorded verbatim, no discussion) → clarification → private ranking/voting. The silent-first design produces more and more-balanced ideas than open discussion. For you, the valuable half is the front: independent silent generation before any evaluation.

**SCAMPER (Bob Eberle, 1971, building on Osborn's question lists).** Substitute, Combine, Adapt, Modify (also Magnify/Minify), Put to another use, Eliminate, Reverse. It is an *idea-mutation checklist*: take an existing item and run it through the seven lenses. Crucially it is iterative — "SCAMPER your SCAMPER results." This is your single best "mutate" operator for the AI loop: take a faction and ask the AI to Substitute its enemy, Reverse its goal, Combine it with another faction, Eliminate its leadership, etc.

**Six Thinking Hats (Edward de Bono, 1985).** Six modes worn one at a time; the **green hat** is dedicated divergent/creative thinking ("possibilities, alternatives, new ideas"), explicitly separated from the black (critical) and white (facts) hats. The principle — "parallel thinking," everyone in the same mode at once — formalizes never mixing generation with criticism. For solo-AI work, "green hat only" is a clean instruction to keep a session generative.

**Random input / forced connections (de Bono's lateral thinking).** Inject a random word/image to force novel associations and break fixation. Practitioners pair this with Crazy Eights using Brian Eno's "Oblique Strategies" cards and report it measurably *increased the breadth* of ideas because each random prompt brought a different bias to the sketch. This is a direct, cheap anti-homogenization lever for AI: feed a random seed word into each generation pass.

**Crazy Eights (Google Design Sprint).** Fold a sheet into 8 panels; sketch 8 ideas in 8 minutes (~40-60 seconds each). The time pressure defeats self-censoring and "pushes beyond your first idea, frequently the least innovative." The transferable principle is the forced quota under time pressure: "give me 8 wildly different X in one pass" mirrors this. (A playful variant, "Evil 8's," asks for the *worst* possible ideas to break performance pressure — a useful de-fixation trick.)

### Area 2 — Morphological analysis & combinatorial generation (your highest-leverage area)

**General Morphological Analysis / the Zwicky box (Fritz Zwicky, Caltech, 1940s).** Steps: (1) frame the problem as an open question; (2) identify independent parameters/axes (5-7 recommended); (3) list values per axis (3-5 each); (4) build the matrix where each cell-combination across axes is one candidate configuration; (5) optionally run a Cross-Consistency Assessment (CCA) to delete combinations that are mutually contradictory; (6) evaluate surviving configurations. A 3-parameter box with 5 values each already yields 75 configurations; real policy studies ran into thousands. The principle: by forcing each idea to take an explicit value on *every* orthogonal axis, you systematically explore a possibility space instead of generating variations on one theme — the exact cure for "samey output."

For factions, your axes might be: **ideology × geography/biome × resource they covet × what they fear × organizing structure × attitude to the player.** Each generated faction is one path through the box. The AI's job is to fill cells and narrate the combination; *your* job is to choose interesting axes and reject incoherent combinations (the CCA step). Related: **attribute listing** and parameter-based "design spaces." This is the most important single technique in this report for your goal — it is the structural backbone that prevents AI homogenization before you even prompt.

### Area 3 — Worldbuilding & faction frameworks from games/TTRPGs

**Blades in the Dark (John Harper) faction system.** Each faction gets a few stats, a **Tier (0-5)** power level, a **Status (-3 to +3)** relationship to others, and one or more **agendas tracked with progress "clocks."** The GM ticks clocks during downtime so the world moves on its own. This is effectively a ready-made morphological schema for a colony-sim: every faction = {power, relationships, agenda, clock}. The design wisdom "make the clock about the obstacle, not the method" maps to defining *what a faction wants*, not how it gets it.

**Apocalypse World (Vincent & Meguey Baker) "Fronts."** A Front is "a collection of threats and agendas, motivated by a Fundamental Scarcity, defined with specialty Moves, and tracked with Countdown Clocks." The GM agenda is "make the world seem real… play to find out what happens." The **Fundamental Scarcity** idea is a strong generative seed: pick what's scarce (water, faith, fuel, labor) and factions/events fall out of it. Note the practitioner critique (The Alexandrian) that in some PbtA games Fronts decay into a mere "idea board" if disconnected from procedure — useful caution that a generation scaffold needs a downstream use.

**Random tables / oracles (e.g., Cezar Capacle's *Random Realities*, Chartopia, solo-RPG oracle zines).** Generative prompt systems organized by category (World, Location, People, Creature, Faction). The critical practitioner distinction (Grumpy Wizard): borrowed tables are treated as "suggestions and idea prompts, not game mechanisms" — you accept or reject results freely. This is exactly the right mental model for AI output: a generator of prompts, with you as the filter. Practitioners already use ChatGPT this way (e.g., "for each letter of the alphabet, generate four names from varied cultures," then filter to ~100).

**Worldbuilding iceberg ("90% underwater").** Generate far more lore than will surface, so what shows has depth behind it — a quantity-first rationale specific to worldbuilding.

### Area 4 — Quantity-first, deferred-judgment idea management

**Tynan Sylvester's RimWorld "ideas reservoir" (GDC 2017, "Contrarian, Ridiculous, and Impossible Game Design Methods").** The Ludeon method: a **massive ideas reservoir** capturing *every* idea, per-person to-do lists, and *regular sorting*. Sylvester lists six advantages, verbatim: "1. Retain inspiration 2. Reduce bias by confirming choices over time 3. Future knowledge advantage 4. No status quo bias 5. Asynchronous work 6. Ideas fight their way to the top." He contrasts this with rigid plans, which suffer "discarded inspiration, discarded feedback, discarded test learning." This is the closest published model to what you want — and it comes from a solo-founded colony-sim studio: capture everything, defer judgment, sort regularly, and let ideas compete *over time* rather than in a single meeting.

**Affinity diagramming / the KJ method (Jiro Kawakita, 1960s).** Put every idea on its own note; cluster by natural similarity (ideally silently, "gut feel," no predetermined categories); label each cluster only after it emerges; then review. It is "the organized output from a brainstorming session," typically handling 40-60 (sometimes 100-200) items. This is your tool for finding *shape and gaps* in a large pool after the fact: cluster 80 generated factions and you instantly see which themes are over-represented and which axis-regions are empty. Notably, the classic ASQ "ZZ-400" case ran it asynchronously over several days on a rarely-used door — a solo-friendly, no-meeting mode.

**Idea reservoirs / backlogs / parking lots** generalize the same principle: a holding space where ideas live without being judged or killed, pruned periodically rather than at capture.

### Area 5 — AI-assisted ideation: the homogenization problem and what actually fixes it

**The named risk: "mode collapse" / "Artificial Hivemind."** Multiple independent LLM ideation sessions converge on similar themes. Jiang et al., *"Artificial Hivemind: The Open-Ended Homogeneity of Language Models (and Beyond)"* (a NeurIPS 2025 Best Paper, spanning 70+ language models — 25 in detail — over a 26K-query dataset), found that for most models the average pairwise similarity among responses to the same prompt exceeded 0.8 in 79% of cases. The effect is both *intra-model* (repeated samples converge) and *inter-model* (independently trained models converge, cross-model cosine similarity ~0.71–0.82), and it persisted under high-entropy sampling (top-p = 0.9, temperature = 1.0); even Min-P sampling, a truncation method designed to support diversity, was insufficient to break the semantic homogeneity. Doshi & Hauser (2024) and others show LLM-generated ideas are collectively *more homogeneous* than independent humans', driven by two mechanisms: individual-level **fixation** (early outputs constrain later ones) and collective-level knowledge aggregation (the model is one averaged distribution, not a population occupying distinct knowledge regions). Risks for you: regression to the mean, anchoring, and a quietly narrowing idea pool.

**What measurably counteracts it (Meincke, Mollick & Terwiesch, "Prompting Diverse Ideas," Wharton/SSRN, Jan 2024; GPT-4, 35 strategies, 1,000 ideas each, diversity = lower average cosine similarity via the Universal Sentence Encoder):**
- **Chain-of-Thought "generate → critique for sameness/boldness → expand" was the best single prompt**, cutting cosine similarity from the base prompt's 0.377 to **0.255** — close to the human-group baseline of **0.243** — and raising the estimated number of unique ideas from **~3,700 to ~4,700**. The winning prompt told the model to list 100 short titles, then "go through the list and determine whether the ideas are different and bold, modify the ideas as needed to make them bolder and more different. No two ideas should be the same. This is important!", then expand each.
- **Personas help moderately** (a "Steve Jobs" persona reached 0.368 vs. 0.377 base).
- **Do NOT seed with example ideas if diversity is the goal:** seeding with prior top ideas *worsened* diversity (0.403-0.432). (Seeding helps idea *quality* in the same group's companion paper, so it's a real trade-off.)
- **Hybrid prompting wins:** overlap between different prompts' pools is low, so generate small pools with several different strategies and combine them — the same reason aggregated independent humans were the most diverse pool.
- **Beware exhaustion:** within one session the diversity edge fades after ~500-750 ideas; prefer many shorter independent runs over one giant batch.
- **Temperature is a weak lever:** in "Examining and Addressing Barriers to Diversity in LLM-Generated Ideas" (arXiv 2602.20408), the LLM default (T = 1.0) produced 22 categories / 88 unique combinations from 990 ideas; raising to T = 1.5 barely moved it (23 / 99), versus humans' 28 categories / 206 unique combinations — and "at temperature 2.0, the model outputs frequently degraded into largely incoherent or garbled text."

**Diverse personas at the input stage (Wan & Kalman, "Diverse AI Personas Can Mitigate the Homogenization Effect," 2025, a modified replication of Doshi & Hauser).** Using ~10 distinct personas (varying culture, thinking style, genre, and creativity tools such as TRIZ/Six Thinking Hats) to generate plots drove *inter-persona* cosine similarity down to ~0.20 (while any single persona's outputs were highly similar, ~0.92), and the move **reversed the homogenization effect** — collective diversity returned to the no-AI human baseline. Their conclusion: "the trade-off may emerge from uniform deployment practices rather than from an inherent limitation of GenAI… diversity can be intentionally built into AI-mediated collaboration." A complementary de-fixation finding (Bordas et al., 2025) is that diversity rises most when you **introduce new, unrelated knowledge** into the ideation — the academic backing for the random-word/forced-connection trick.

**The human-in-the-loop model.** Practitioner and academic writing converges on "augmentation, not replacement": the AI generates abundant raw ideas (ideation is now effectively free), and the experienced human applies *selection pressure* — keep/kill/merge — as the diversity filter. Co-creation works best iteratively, looping back to ask for more when stuck rather than proceeding linearly; targeted re-generation ("AI upsampling") of a near-miss is a recognized move.

### Area 6 — The generation-to-decision handoff

**Double Diamond (UK Design Council, 2005, rooted in Osborn-Parnes and Béla Bánáthy's divergence-convergence model).** Two diamonds, each a diverge-then-converge: Discover→Define (problem), Develop→Deliver (solution). The governing principle: "separate the generation of ideas from the selection of ideas… trying to diverge and converge at the same time is like driving with the brakes on."

**Osborn-Parnes Creative Problem Solving (CPS).** The crucial structural insight: *every* stage alternates a divergent phase then a convergent phase — and across the whole process you repeatedly cycle generation→selection rather than doing it once. Modern CPS (Creative Education Foundation; Isaksen & Treffinger) groups this as Clarify→Ideate→Develop→Implement, each with its own diverge/converge pulse.

**The practical promotion trigger.** Nothing in a brainstorm pool is a decision until options start *competing for the same slot*. While ideas merely accumulate, they coexist in the reservoir (Sylvester: "ideas fight their way to the top" *over time*). The moment two pooled factions can't both ship, or a building type must occupy a fixed UI slot, that item has become a decision — promote it out of the pool and into your ADR process for structured evaluation. The pool is the divergent diamond; the ADR is the convergent one.

## Recommendations

**A concrete lightweight loop (the "Generation Log," parallel to but separate from your ADRs):**

1. **Define the morphological frame first (5 min).** Before generating any content type, write 4-6 orthogonal axes and 3-5 values each. Factions: ideology × biome × coveted resource × deepest fear × structure × stance toward player. Events: trigger × scale × system-touched × player-agency × tone. This is your anti-samey backbone and your AI prompt skeleton — and the highest-leverage habit in this whole list.
2. **Generate quantity-first with green-hat-only framing using Chain-of-Thought.** Prompt the AI: "Generate 30 short faction titles spanning these axes; then review the list and make them bolder and more different — no two should be the same; then expand each to 2 sentences." This mirrors the single best-validated diversity prompt. Defer all judgment in this step.
3. **Run 3-4 *different* passes and pool them, not one giant pass.** Vary the lens each pass — a different persona ("as a cynical historian," "as a hopeful colonist"), or a random seed word (forced connection / de-fixation). Combine outputs. This is hybrid prompting plus diverse personas, the two strongest measured anti-homogenization moves. Avoid pasting your existing favorites in as examples (it reduces diversity).
4. **Capture everything into one reservoir.** A single running doc/spreadsheet (or Notion DB) per content type. Nothing is rejected at capture. Tag each item with its axis-values so gaps are visible.
5. **Iterate with keep/kill/mutate/merge.** React to the pool: KEEP the sparks; KILL nothing permanently (move to a "cold storage" tab — this preserves Sylvester's "no status quo bias" advantage); MUTATE the promising ones by running them through SCAMPER (ask the AI to Reverse the goal / Substitute the enemy / Combine two factions); MERGE near-duplicates. Feed your reactions back so the AI regenerates around what excited you.
6. **Cluster periodically with affinity mapping.** Every ~40-60 items, cluster by theme (you can even have the AI propose clusters, then you correct). Name clusters only after they emerge. This reveals over-represented themes and empty axis-regions to target next.
7. **Promote on competition, not on quality.** When two pooled items vie for the same slot, that's the handoff: open an ADR and evaluate convergently. Until then, let them coexist in the reservoir.

**Tooling note for a solo dev:** keep the Generation Log and the ADR log as physically separate artifacts so the *mode* stays unambiguous — reservoir = judgment off, ADR = judgment on.

**Benchmarks that would change the approach:**
- If a pass returns visibly samey output → you skipped the morphological frame or seeded with examples; re-frame axes and strip example ideas.
- If clustering shows one mega-cluster → your axes aren't truly orthogonal; pick axes that vary independently.
- If you're stalling on "which is best" inside the pool → you've drifted into convergence; stop, promote the contested item to an ADR, and resume generating.
- If the AI feels exhausted/repetitive late in a session → you're past the ~500-750-idea diversity cliff; start a fresh session with a new lens/persona rather than pushing the same thread.

## Caveats
- **Diversity metrics are imperfect.** The cosine-similarity findings (Meincke et al.) are GPT-4-specific and the authors caution that diversity can be bought by sacrificing quality — more diverse ≠ a better *single* best idea. Treat the numbers as directional.
- **The persona/homogenization reversal rests on a modest sample** (Wan & Kalman, n≈97; the diversity-restoration result was statistically non-significant in its sign-reversal, p≈0.15-0.35). The robust claim is "diversity preserved to the human baseline," not "diversity exceeded humans."
- **Classic group-brainstorming research warns that open group ideation underperforms independent work.** This is an argument *for* your solo + structured-AI setup, but also a reminder that the AI can re-introduce "groupthink-like" convergence (it anchors on its own earlier output) if you let it run one long unbroken thread.
- **TTRPG/de Bono tools have thin formal efficacy evidence.** Six Thinking Hats in particular has "sparse research evidence" for generalized improvement; these are useful scaffolds and heuristics, not proven instruments. Use them for structure, not as guarantees.
- **Morphological combinatorics explodes fast.** 6 axes × 4 values = 4,096 cells; you are not meant to enumerate all of them — sample interesting paths and use Cross-Consistency to prune incoherent ones.
- **A generation scaffold needs a downstream use or it rots** (the PbtA "Fronts as dead idea board" failure mode). The promotion-to-ADR step is what keeps your reservoir a living pipeline rather than a graveyard.
